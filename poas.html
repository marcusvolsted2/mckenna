POAS vs ROAS: Why Profit Beats Revenue

ROAS tells you how much revenue your ads generate. POAS tells you how much money you actually make. Here's why that difference matters more than you think.

The Problem With ROAS

ROAS - Return on Ad Spend - was originally meant to show profit returns from advertising. But in practice, it became "Revenue on Ad Spend" because that was the data readily available to platforms like Google and Meta.

What ROAS misses

When you optimize for ROAS, you're optimizing for top-line revenue. But revenue is not profit. A campaign can show a ROAS of 10x and still lose you money if the products sold have thin margins, high shipping costs, or were sold at a discount.

A real-world example

Consider two products in the same catalog:

If you set a single ROAS target of 10, the frying pan campaign gets shut down - even though it's highly profitable. With POAS, every order above 1.0 is confirmed profitable, regardless of product price. The three campaigns that ROAS would have killed were actually the most profitable ones.

What Is POAS?

POAS - Profit on Ad Spend - is a metric that divides gross profit attributable to a marketing channel by the ad spend on that channel. It's simple: if your POAS is above 1.0, you're making money. Below 1.0, you're losing money.

The Three Traditional ROAS Tactics (and Why They Fail)

Agencies have developed workarounds to compensate for ROAS's limitations. But each one introduces new risks.

1

Average ROAS Target

Uses average product margins across a year of history to set one ROAS target for everything.

Pitfall: Accepts unavoidable losses on low-margin products. Risks shutting down profitable low-price, high-margin products. Requires continuous validation that never happens.

2

Differentiated ROAS Strategy

Divides products into multiple groups with different ROAS targets (typically three tiers).

Pitfall: People don't always buy the product they clicked on. A low-ROAS ad can drive a high-margin sale that goes undetected, creating attribution blindness.

3

Highest Break-Even ROAS

Sets the target at the highest break-even ROAS in the catalog to ensure no product loses money.

Pitfall: Unnecessarily suppresses order volume for products with lower break-even requirements. You leave profitable sales on the table.
